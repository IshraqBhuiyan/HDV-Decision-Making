Your name is Ronald McDonald and you are a pilot. Today you are the Captain of Disunited Airlines Flight 91 from New York to Florida.

Here is the story of one fateful day for you.

Next
Heavy snow temporarily closed the airport, but now it's finally open again. You really want to get out of this frozen hell and be back under the warm Florida Sun. You haven't seen your family in so long too. You'd really like to get back as fast as possible. It's time to board your airplane.
You arrive in the cockpit of the Boeing 737 assigned to you with your copilot.
You begin the preflight checkist. You: Engines?
Copilot: Check.
You: Fuel?
Copilot: Check.
You: Tire Pressure?
Copilot: Check.

Have they deiced the plane yet? It seems that the airport deicing crew is tending to it right now. It should be done within 15 to 20 minutes.

The weather outside is gradually worsening. If you don't take off soon, you may very well get grounded again. you really want to get back to Florida and have some time with my family before your next flight. You rarely see them nowadays because of the back to back flights. The passengers are also extremely tired and anxious. You don't want to be the one telling them you can't take off and then deal with their complaints.
After some time the plane is deiced.

Air traffic control has given you the clearance to taxi to the runway. Unfortunately, because of weather conditions and cleanup, only one runway is open. Ahead of you there are many other planes waiting to take off. It will take a while to get clearance for takeoff.

After 40 minutes of waiting, you copilot turns to you and says,
"Hey Cap, that sure is a lot of snow and ice that's built up on our wings. You sure you don't want to turn back for another deicing?"

Looking at the wings, you see that there is indeed snow piled up on the wings, and according to standard procedure, you should be turning back for a deicing in order to ensure the snow doesn't affect the lift the wings give.

You can let Air Traffic Control know and turn back to deice, which is standard procedure and the safest option. But you also really want to get out of here ASAP. The sooner you take off, the sooner you can fly above the storm clouds and not worry about this crappy weather anymore. The snow on the wings is a problem, but perhaps a deicing isn't necessary to melt it. There's another jet in front of you, and if you go a little closer to it, the heat from its jet engines should melt the snow on your wings, right? This isn't standard procedure, but it may just work. Also, the passengers are getting more and more frustrated the more time it takes to get into the air. The faster you get in the air, the less likely you are to deal with them. Angry passengers are truly frightening, after all!
So what do you do: Do you follow standard procedure and turn back for a deicing, or do you try to use the heat from the jet in front of you to deice and continue?

You decide to listen to your copilot and give the instrument panel a look. Looking at it, the readings really do look weird. Quickly, you slow down the airplane in order to stop it from taking off. You notify Air Traffic Control something is wrong, and turn your airplane around.
Next

Good job!
You turned around, following standard procedures, and didn't ignore your copilot completely! Your plane was thoroughly deiced, and although it was delayed even further and you had to face the onslaught of the angry passengers, you made the responsible decision! During the deicing, you also discover an equipment failure due to you not turning on the airplane deicing, causing some of the sensors to behave improperly. Your actions have saved you, your crew and the passengers from a terrible potential accident. You should be proud of yourself!

The airplane ascends a few hundred feet. You're relieved you're finally in the air. Now you can be home soon.
All of a sudden after a few seconds after takeoff, the stick shaker and alarm go off, indicating that the airplane is in danger of stalling.
You and your copilot try to get the airplane to start climbing and to get out of the stall.
But despite your efforts, you can't prevent it. The airplane starts stalling and falling!
There's nothing you can do! Your airplane continues falling and crashes into a bridge below.
You, everyone on board, and a few motorists unfortunate to be on the bridge die and many other motorists on the bridge are injured.
The decisions you have made throughout this flight, such as ignoring standard procedures and your copilot, have lead to this unfortunate accident.
